Bottle

The bottle is the item that the user will interact with the most and carry throughout the day with them. Our design considers the average daily handling of a water bottle and the needs of someone spending most of their time traveling around the city. The bottle needs to be durable enough to withstand a few drops while maintaining a lightweight so it is not a burden when carried throughout the day. Besides consideration of the user’s experience with the bottle, the material we select must comply with one of our main goals of decreasing environmental impact. With all this in mind and after consideration of a variety of materials, we narrowed down our options to stainless steel and glass. With further surveys and interactions with our users we will be able to settle on a design and a material that will fit their needs and hopefully increase usage of the bottle once we launch our system. In the future, if our system is implemented, we hope to work with a variety of designers and local non-profit organizations to create special limited edition bottles. This might entice more users and bring awareness to the non-profits.

Business Plan

One of the core goals of this shared system is to extend existing infrastructure and create a resource that is accessible for all, without the elitism that can surround some environmental initiatives. Our system would have a free water dispensing option that anyone could use, ensuring equitable water access; in addition to that, we would have a subscription plan available for those who would like advanced features. Similar to farmers’ markets, there would be an option for SNAP/EBT cards as well.

Monthly subscriptions would be available, especially for visitors to the city, and a yearly plan would offer a discount on the monthly plan and cost. For instance, if a monthly subscription plan was $3 each month, a yearly plan would cost $24, about the same as a reusable water bottle. While single-use water bottle use varies, spending a conservative $1 for 1 bottle of water every day would result in $365 of water bottle purchases each year, significantly more than a yearly subscription fee. Initial costs would include installation of the water dispensing systems and bottles; ongoing costs would be system maintenance.

We would partner with grassroots community organizations throughout the process to ensure that our project is truly contributing to the residents of the city and not exacerbating issues like gentrification. Ideally, we would like to create a public-private partnership with New York City agencies like the Department of Environmental Protection, Department of Transportation, and Department of Health. This would allow for a more widespread reach of our systems throughout the city. Outreach could include educational booths at farmers’ markets, schools and universities, street fairs, and potentially at programs like NYCHA Green Corps. In addition, we could use both online advertising and public bus stations, libraries, and more; ideally our system would eventually be incorporated into maps of public resources. We hope that the system would catch on in New York City and eventually spread to other cities around the world, adapted to their local water system and needs.

Community Information

Education often times is as important as recycling itself. Our main target audience; people who care about the environment, recycle for the purpose of sustainability and others who are visiting the city or want to participate out of curiosity should be educated on what they are consuming and how they are affecting the environment.

Understanding why and how we are recycling, cleaning, and reusing provides transparency and educates people on our base values and target goals. Providing statistics on how much less waste we are creating in comparison to the numbers provided by the city are all part of providing people with the knowledge on how we are impacting the environment. Part of the reason water fountains, water filtration systems and NYC tap water is not trusted is the lack of knowledge on the chemical components in the water. Another aspect of our transparent goal is by providing people with DEP safe drinking water requirements in comparison to our quarterly water testing results. Bottled water based on FDA regulations is not tested as often as tap water, with this in mind our main goal is to continuously test the water we are providing to the people. The easiest form of education is by having pictures and posters at locations in the city with the most foot traffic, bus stops and subway stations. For the population who would like to do further research, they will be able to find more information on our website and videos of how it all came together.
